## Register

**product** — the design serves the task. The user is here to study, not to
admire the interface.

## Users

A solo CompTIA Security+ candidate, usually studying alone in the evening on a
laptop or curled up on a couch with a phone. They've been at it for weeks. They
already know the general shape of the exam. What they need from this site:

- Drill questions quickly without ceremony.
- Get an unambiguous **right / wrong** verdict the moment they click an answer.
- See a one-paragraph explanation that makes the *why* stick — not a wall of
  text.
- Track their own progress across the session so they can feel themselves
  getting better.

They are *not* here to share their score, customize a theme, or read a brand
manifesto. The tool should disappear into the task.

## Product purpose

A locally-run practice quiz over a fixed pool of 604 SY0-701 questions. One
session = one randomized 60–90 question quiz. Answers reveal instantly with
context. Results screen reviews misses with the explanation attached.

## Tone

Quiet, honest, instructive. No celebration confetti, no streak guilt-trips, no
"You crushed it!" copy. Verdicts are factual ("Correct — B is right." /
"Not quite — you picked D, correct answer is B."). Explanations read like a
patient TA, not a marketing email.

Specifically avoid:

- Marketing voice ("Master the exam!", "Crush your study session!").
- Gamification noise (XP, badges, streak emojis).
- Modal-stacked "Are you sure?"-style nags.

## Anti-references

What this product is **not** trying to look or feel like:

- Duolingo / Brilliant — bright, gamified, character-led. We're a study tool,
  not a daily-engagement loop.
- Quizlet flashcard skins — generic SaaS card grids and rainbow accents.
- Generic AI-purple gradients on every surface. Anti-cliché.
- "Dashboard with hero metric + gradient + supporting stats" SaaS template.

## Strategic principles

1. **Verdict legibility beats decoration.** The single most important moment
   is the half-second after a tap — make it impossible to misread whether the
   answer was right or wrong. Color, text, position all align.
2. **Density follows attention.** Question + four options on screen at once.
   Don't push the options below the fold to make room for chrome.
3. **Mobile is a primary mode.** A studier reaches for their phone in line at
   coffee. 44px tap targets, full-width buttons, no horizontal scroll.
4. **The review screen is the lesson.** Most learning happens between quizzes.
   The "missed questions" review must be where the explanation feels prominent,
   not buried under the score.
5. **No engagement bait.** No streaks, no notifications, no emails. The user
   owns the schedule.
